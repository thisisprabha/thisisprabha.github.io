The Definition

AI-Native Doesn't Mean "Has AI." It Means Rebuilt for It.

Most tools are AI-assisted. A technician uses them. A few are different. The agent uses the tool itself. That's the gap. That's what we're building at SuperOps.

Vendors Call It AI

AI-Assisted IT

You file a ticket. A copilot suggests knowledge base articles. A human still reads it, routes it, fixes it. The AI is a widget on a dashboard. It writes your email drafts. It summarizes your meetings. It doesn't touch anything real.

This Is Different

AI-Native IT

You slack the agent. It asks what it needs to know. It figures out what's wrong. It resets your password, installs your software, provisions your account, updates your ticket. Then it tells you what it did. The human reviews. The agent executes.

The Technical Divide

What "Native" Actually Looks Like

Here's the architecture conversation when someone asks "but couldn't X add AI too?"

Their Architecture

Assisted Model

Human → UI → LLM → Suggestion

The human decides whether to act. The LLM is a consultant in the loop. Workflows are designed for human execution. AI is a layer on top.

Our Architecture

Native Model

Human Goal → LLM (ReAct Loop) → Tool Execution → Result Audit

The human sets the goal. The LLM reasons through the approach. The agent executes. The human audits. The playbook structure is designed for machine consumption.

The Test: Can a Human Walk Away?

If you can step away from the keyboard and the system keeps working — diagnosis, action, reporting — it's native. If you have to babysit every suggestion, it's assisted. Most "AI" IT products fail this test. MSPclaw doesn't.
